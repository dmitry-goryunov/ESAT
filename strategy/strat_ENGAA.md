# ENGAA Strategy, relevant sections for ESAT Maths and Physics

Scope reviewed: ENGAA Section 1 papers available in the project folder, 2016 to 2023.  
Use only the parts that overlap with ESAT Maths and Physics. Treat Advanced Maths/Physics questions as extension drills, not as first-pass ESAT calibration.

---

## 1. Format and timing

### 2016 to 2019 style

- Section 1: 80 minutes.
- 54 multiple-choice questions.
- Part A: Mathematics and Physics, 28 questions.
- Part B: Advanced Mathematics and Advanced Physics, 26 questions.
- No calculator.
- No penalty for wrong answers.

ESAT-style timing:

| Block | Questions | Time | Time per question |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Part A | 28 | 40 to 42 min | about 1 min 30 sec |
| Part B relevant subset | 12 to 18 | 20 to 27 min | about 1 min 30 sec |
| Review/fill blanks | open | 3 to 5 min | protect easy marks |

### 2020 to 2023 style

- Section 1: 60 minutes.
- 40 multiple-choice questions.
- Part A: Mathematics and Physics, 20 questions.
- Part B: Advanced Mathematics and Advanced Physics, 20 questions.
- The paper advises 30 minutes per part.
- No penalty for wrong answers.

ESAT-style timing:

| Block | Questions | Time | Time per question |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Part A | 20 | 28 to 30 min | 1 min 24 sec to 1 min 30 sec |
| Part B relevant subset | 10 to 15 | 15 to 22 min | 1 min 30 sec |
| Review/fill blanks | open | 2 to 4 min | no blanks |

## 2. What to use from ENGAA

High relevance for ESAT:
- Part A Maths and Physics.
- Part B mechanics, circuits, waves, thermal, energy, graphs, sequences, quadratics, trigonometry and coordinate geometry.

Lower priority:
- Very calculus-heavy questions.
- Advanced integration/differentiation questions not in ESAT specification.
- Overly long proof-style algebra.

Rule: use ENGAA as a **speed and mixed-topic recognition drill**, not as a pure content benchmark.

## 3. ENGAA quick-technique map

| Question type | Fast technique |
|---|---|
| Mixed Maths/Physics alternation | Reset method after each question; do not carry a maths mindset into physics |
| Scaling laws | Use proportionality before numbers |
| Nuclear decay/fission | A/Z ledger first |
| Circuits | Reduce circuit first, then current, then power/voltage |
| Energy/power | Input/useful/wasted audit |
| Wave particle distance | One oscillation means 4A particle travel |
| Velocity-time graphs | Area = displacement; total distance uses absolute area |
| Bearings/geometry | Redraw cleanly, add North lines |
| Symbolic rearrangement | Substitute options or test dimensions |
| Advanced mechanics | Free-body diagram and resultant force first |
| Moments/equilibrium | Choose pivot to kill unknown forces |
| Collisions | Momentum first, energy loss second |
| Similar shapes | length k, area k², volume k³ |
| Logs/exponents | Convert to common base |
| Quadratics/root conditions | Discriminant or completing square |

## 4. Year-by-year strategy notes

### ENGAA 2016

Pattern:
- Strong ESAT relevance.
- Part A alternates basic Maths and Physics under pressure.
- Frequent themes: inequalities, ratios, graph meaning, nuclear decay, fission, circuits, depreciation, star scaling, bearings, wave particle motion, proportionality, pulleys, trapezium area, density and thermal transfer.
- Part B has excellent extension mechanics: Newton's third law, tension, work done, moments, projectile motion, collisions, friction and spacecraft momentum.

Best techniques:
- Use A/Z ledger for all nuclear questions.
- Use proportionality for star radiation and inverse-square/root relations.
- For wave particle questions, use `distance per cycle = 4 × amplitude`.
- For circuits, find current first and then resistor-specific power.
- For velocity-time graph, separate total distance from displacement.
- For pulleys and tensions, isolate one object at a time.

Priority questions:
- Part A: Q2, Q4, Q6, Q8, Q10, Q12, Q14, Q16, Q20, Q22, Q23, Q28.
- Part B: Q30, Q32, Q34, Q36, Q38, Q40, Q44, Q46, Q48, Q50, Q52, Q54.

---

### ENGAA 2017

Pattern:
- Similar to 2016, but with more algebraic manipulation and graph/geometry traps.
- Good for mixed Maths/Physics switching.
- Useful for ESAT: proportionality, circuits, energy, kinematics, waves, transformations and standard mechanics.

Best techniques:
- For algebraic options, test simple values before expanding.
- For proportionality, identify powers first.
- For mechanics, draw forces and use resultant force.
- For graph questions, ask area or gradient before formulae.
- Use statement-by-statement truth tables.

Priority:
- All Part A questions are useful.
- In Part B, use mechanics/circuits/waves/questions with familiar ESAT content; skip heavy calculus on first pass.

---

### ENGAA 2018

Pattern:
- Strong in combined algebra, coordinate geometry, energy conversion, waves, electricity and graph interpretation.
- Engineering flavour increases: work, power, resistance, forces, moments.

Best techniques:
- Use energy audit for all machine/efficiency questions.
- Use circuit reduction before calculation.
- Use gradients for graph questions.
- Use symmetry or coordinates for geometry.
- Use answer substitution on polynomial/factor questions.

Priority:
- Part A for timed ESAT drill.
- Part B mechanics and circuits as harder follow-up.

---

### ENGAA 2019

Pattern:
- Good bridge from NSAA to ESAT.
- More compact modern question style.
- Frequent traps: units, powers of ten, graph interpretation, proportionality, algebraic rearrangement.

Best techniques:
- Units first for Physics.
- Powers-of-ten bookkeeping for numerical Physics.
- Complete square or discriminant for quadratics.
- Table method for probability or multi-category data.
- Free-body diagram for any mechanics question with contact/tension/friction.

Priority:
- Use Part A under strict 30 to 35 minute timing.
- Use selected Part B mechanics, circuits, waves and geometry only.

---

### ENGAA 2020

Pattern:
- 60-minute modern format, closer to ESAT pressure.
- 40 questions, recommended 30 minutes per part.
- More intensive switching between short Maths and Physics problems.

Best techniques:
- 20-second route test becomes essential.
- Skip heavy Part B questions on first pass.
- For Physics, use formula recognition and unit checks.
- For Maths, use substitution and special values.
- For graph questions, mark area/gradient/intercept immediately.

Priority:
- Part A full run is high value.
- Part B selected questions only unless doing a hard mock.

---

### ENGAA 2021

Pattern:
- Modern style: shorter stems but high trap density.
- Useful for ESAT because it mixes standard maths fluency with physics calculation.

Best techniques:
- For ratios/percentages, use multipliers.
- For circuits, fixed-voltage constraints matter.
- For transformers, power conservation is usually faster than current ratios.
- For kinematics, use `v² = u² + 2as` when time is absent.
- For similar shapes, do not confuse surface area and volume scaling.

Priority:
- Part A first.
- Part B mechanics, springs, energy, waves and circuits as extension.

---

### ENGAA 2022

Pattern:
- Very close to NSAA 2022 overlap in several Maths/Physics styles.
- High-value topics: algebraic simplification, geometry, inverse proportionality, work/energy, circuits, ultrasound, graphs and thermal transfer.

Best techniques:
- For ultrasound/echo: halve travel path.
- For work against resistance: useful work versus energy lost.
- For spring scaling: energy proportional to extension squared.
- For mean/median: write ordered list explicitly.
- For similar triangles: matching sides in matching order.

Priority:
- Part A is high priority.
- Part B only if it maps directly to ESAT specification.

---

### ENGAA 2023

Pattern:
- 40 questions in 60 minutes, explicitly 30 minutes per part.
- Part A includes surface area scaling, stopping distance, rearrangement, circuits, coordinate geometry, spring energy graph, exponents, density/pressure, percentages, half-life, average speed, layered conduction, similar volume scaling, transformers, scientific notation, wave particle speed, vectors, circuit power, trig optimisation and gas compression.
- Part B contains extension but still useful topics: factors, stationary waves, sequences, projectile impulse, geometric progressions, friction power, circle theorems, circuit networks, completing square, equilibrium, blocks and contact forces, log equations, resistance scaling, binomial terms, interference and series springs.

Best techniques:
- For stopping distance: work-energy beats SUVAT.
- For spring graph: connect `E = 1/2 Fx` and `E ∝ x²`.
- For layered conduction: poorer conductor has steeper temperature drop.
- For gas compression: use pressure ratios, not linear depth intuition.
- For stationary waves: closed-open pipe fundamental is quarter-wave; open-open is half-wave.
- For resistance reshaping: same volume means area changes when length changes.

Priority questions:
- Part A: Q1 to Q20 all high value.
- Part B: Q21, Q22, Q24, Q26, Q28, Q30, Q32, Q34, Q36, Q38, Q40.

## 5. Best ENGAA strategies

### Strategy 1: Mixed reset

ENGAA changes topic almost every question. After each answer, mentally reset.

Use:
`Maths? Physics? Graph? Circuit? Energy? Units?`

This prevents using the wrong method.

### Strategy 2: Physics first filter

For every Physics question:

1. What quantity is asked?
2. What units must the answer have?
3. Is the relationship proportional, graphical or conservation-based?
4. Can I eliminate before calculating?

### Strategy 3: Advanced Part B filter

For Part B, classify each question:

| Type | Action |
|---|---|
| Mechanics / circuits / waves / thermal / energy | attempt |
| Standard quadratics / sequences / logs / graphs | attempt |
| Long calculus / advanced algebra | mark for later |
| Unfamiliar proof-style question | skip first pass |

### Strategy 4: No blank answers

ENGAA has no penalty. Always answer every question, even if it is an educated guess.
