# NSAA Maths and Physics Strategy, 2016 to 2023

Purpose: use NSAA Section 1 as an ESAT-style speed and technique training source. The emphasis is not elegant solutions. The emphasis is fast recognition, safe elimination and disciplined timing.

---

## 1. Format and timing

### 2016 to 2019 format

- Section 1: 80 minutes.
- Part A Mathematics is compulsory.
- Candidates then complete two further parts.
- Each part has 18 multiple-choice questions.
- For a Maths and Physics drill: Part A plus Part B gives 36 questions.

Recommended ESAT-style timing:

| Block | Questions | Time | Time per question |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Part A Maths | 18 | 25 to 27 min | 1 min 25 sec to 1 min 30 sec |
| Part B Physics | 18 | 27 to 30 min | 1 min 30 sec to 1 min 40 sec |
| Review buffer | open | 2 to 5 min | use only for marked questions |

### 2020 to 2023 format

- Section 1: 60 minutes.
- Part A Mathematics is compulsory.
- Candidates then complete one further part.
- Each part has 20 multiple-choice questions.
- For a Maths and Physics drill: Part A plus Part B gives 40 questions.

Recommended ESAT-style timing:

| Block | Questions | Time | Time per question |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Part A Maths | 20 | 28 to 30 min | 1 min 24 sec to 1 min 30 sec |
| Part B Physics | 20 | 30 to 32 min | 1 min 30 sec to 1 min 36 sec |

No penalty means every question must be answered.

## 2. Universal NSAA strategy

Use a three-pass structure.

### Pass 1: collect quick marks

Spend no more than 45 to 60 seconds on any question unless the route is completely clear.

Immediate-solve questions include:
- simple rearrangements
- direct percentage multipliers
- simple ratio scaling
- direct circuit current/resistance/power
- direct nuclear A/Z balance
- obvious graph area or gradient

### Pass 2: medium questions

Return to questions where the method is known but arithmetic is longer.

Typical medium questions:
- probability without replacement
- bearings and trigonometry
- multi-stage energy accounting
- circuit reductions
- thermal calculations
- mixed half-life problems

### Pass 3: long or trap-heavy questions

Only attempt properly if time remains.

Typical long questions:
- awkward geometry with multiple hidden triangles
- statement-combination questions
- unfamiliar diagrams
- algebra with many symbols
- multi-step probability
- forces with several bodies or constraints

## 3. First 15-second classification

Before solving, label the question mentally.

| Label | Question type | First technique |
|---|---|---|
| A | Algebra | Factor, substitute or compare coefficients |
| R | Ratio / percentage | Use multipliers or choose a convenient total |
| G | Geometry | Draw a clean diagram and mark invariants |
| P | Probability | Count outcomes or use complement |
| T | Table / statements | Evaluate each statement independently |
| V | Graph | Identify gradient, area, intercept or asymptote |
| C | Circuit | Reduce the circuit and find current first |
| N | Nuclear | Balance mass number and proton number |
| E | Energy | Input, useful, wasted and stored energy audit |
| W | Waves | Decide whether it is wave speed or particle motion |
| F | Forces | Draw a free-body diagram and use resultant force |

If the label is unclear after 15 seconds, skip and return.

## 4. Maths quick-technique map

### Algebra and rearrangement

Recurring years: all years, especially 2016, 2017, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023.

Best techniques:
- Substitute answer options for awkward rearrangements.
- Test simple values such as x = 1 if options are algebraic.
- Factor before expanding.
- For inequalities, solve normally then check one test value.
- For roots of quadratics, use sum/product or discriminant before full formula where possible.

Common traps:
- Sign reversal in inequalities.
- Losing the ± after square-rooting.
- Expanding when cancellation is available.
- Confusing positive difference between roots with sum of roots.

### Ratio, percentages and proportionality

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Use multipliers, not additive language.
- Reverse percentages by dividing by the multiplier.
- For direct/inverse proportionality, write proportional relation first.
- For combined ratios, make the shared quantity match.
- For similar shapes: length scale k, area scale k², volume scale k³.

Common traps:
- Increasing by 20% then decreasing by 20% is not back to the start.
- 700% more means 8 times original, not 7 times.
- Average speed is total distance divided by total time, not average of speeds.

### Probability

Recurring years: 2017 to 2023.

Best techniques:
- Use complement for “at least one”.
- Use a two-way table for gender/activity or box/colour questions.
- Count ordered outcomes when dice or repeated draws are involved.
- For without replacement, reduce numerator and denominator after each draw.

Common traps:
- Treating unordered and ordered outcomes interchangeably.
- Forgetting the selection probability of the box or first stage.
- Confusing “more green than red” with a fixed number of greens.

### Geometry and trigonometry

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Redraw diagrams cleanly.
- Mark right angles, equal lengths, parallel lines and tangents first.
- Use area formulae before coordinate-heavy methods.
- In bearings, draw North lines and convert to triangle angles.
- For similar triangles, write matching sides in the same order.
- For circles, use radius to tangent is perpendicular.

Common traps:
- Diagrams are not to scale.
- Bearing angles are measured clockwise from North.
- Surface area and volume scale differently.
- Diagonal length in 3D is not a face diagonal.

### Graphs, functions and transformations

Recurring years: 2019 to 2023 most strongly.

Best techniques:
- Track one point through a transformation.
- For tangent to a circle, radius gradient × tangent gradient = -1.
- For minimum of a quadratic, complete the square.
- For counting solutions, sketch both sides rather than solve exactly.
- For transformations, apply operations in order, not all at once.

Common traps:
- Reflection after rotation changes signs in a non-intuitive way.
- “Must be true” statistics questions require counterexamples.
- For tan x = kx style questions, graph shape beats algebra.

## 5. Physics quick-technique map

### Units and dimensions

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Check final units before detailed calculation.
- Use dimensional shortcuts: power = J s^-1, pressure = N m^-2, density = mass/volume, charge = current × time.
- For symbolic answers, remove dimensionally impossible options first.

Common traps:
- cm to m and g to kg.
- Minutes to seconds.
- cm³ to m³.
- Confusing mass density with weight density.

### Graphs

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Velocity-time graph: area = displacement, area below axis is negative displacement but positive distance.
- Speed-time graph: area = distance.
- Distance-time graph: gradient = speed.
- Momentum-time graph: gradient = resultant force.
- Force-distance graph: area = work done.
- V-I graph: gradient is resistance only when V is vertical and I is horizontal.

Common traps:
- Total distance versus displacement.
- Average speed versus average velocity.
- Taking a gradient when the question asks for area.
- Reading nonlinear graphs as if they are straight lines.

### Energy, power and efficiency

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Write an energy audit:
  input energy = useful energy + wasted energy.
- Use efficiency = useful output / input.
- Use power = energy / time.
- For lifting: useful energy = mgh.
- For heating: energy = mcΔT, plus latent heat if there is melting.

Common traps:
- Confusing wasted energy with input minus useful mechanical gain.
- Forgetting efficiency before or after power/time calculation.
- Using total distance instead of vertical height for gravitational potential energy.

### Circuits

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Find equivalent resistance first.
- In series: same current.
- In parallel: same potential difference.
- Use P = IV, I²R or V²/R depending on which quantities are known.
- For variable or thermistor questions, find the fixed resistor from the first condition, then reuse it.

Common traps:
- Treating voltage as fixed across one resistor in a changing series circuit.
- Forgetting that total power changes when total resistance changes at fixed supply voltage.
- Misreading V-I graph axes.

### Waves and electromagnetic radiation

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Always start with v = fλ.
- In a new medium, frequency usually stays constant.
- For particle motion in a transverse wave: one cycle means distance travelled by particle = 4 × amplitude.
- For echoes and ultrasound: reflected path is there and back.
- For Doppler/redshift: moving away means lower frequency and longer wavelength.

Common traps:
- Confusing wave speed with particle speed.
- Using amplitude as wavelength.
- Forgetting the factor of two in echo/ultrasound travel.

### Nuclear physics

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Make an A/Z ledger.
- Alpha: mass number -4, proton number -2.
- Beta minus: mass number unchanged, proton number +1.
- In fission, balance total mass number and proton number.
- For half-life with background count, subtract background before halving.

Common traps:
- Using measured count rate instead of corrected count rate.
- Forgetting that beta minus increases proton number.
- Assuming daughter isotope mass equals parent mass after beta decay.

### Mechanics, forces and momentum

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- Draw a free-body diagram.
- Use resultant force, not just applied force.
- For collisions, choose a positive direction and keep signs.
- For impulse: force × time = change in momentum.
- For inelastic collisions, momentum is conserved, kinetic energy is not.
- For constant acceleration, use v² = u² + 2as when time is not needed.

Common traps:
- Treating Newton’s third-law pairs as forces on the same object.
- Ignoring resistance/friction when asked for average force or work done against drag.
- Forgetting weight still acts during contact/collision unless explicitly negligible.

### Thermal physics and gases

Recurring years: all years.

Best techniques:
- For conduction: rate ∝ area × temperature difference / length.
- For radiation: dull black surfaces emit and absorb more than shiny surfaces.
- For ideal gas at constant temperature: pressure ∝ density.
- For mixed hot/cold water or ice: heat lost = heat gained + latent heat.

Common traps:
- Temperature difference matters, not absolute temperature.
- Vacuum stops conduction/convection, not radiation.
- Shiny surfaces are poor absorbers and poor emitters.

## 6. Year-by-year strategy notes

### NSAA 2016

Maths pattern:
- Foundation-heavy but trap-rich: inequalities, surds, ratios, means, depreciation, bearings, inverse proportionality, trapezium area, formula rearrangement, probability and geometry.

Best Maths techniques:
- Use table for probability and mixtures.
- Use multipliers for depreciation.
- Use shared ratio matching.
- For bearings, draw North at each point.
- For formula rearrangement, substitute an option if algebra is messy.

Physics pattern:
- Very useful ESAT-style physics: EM redshift, nuclear decay, conduction scaling, graph matching, fission balance, circuits, wave particle distance, pulley tension, density, thermal radiation/convection, velocity-time graph, half-life and echoes.

Best Physics techniques:
- A/Z ledger for nuclear questions.
- Scaling before calculation for conduction and star radiation.
- Circuit current first.
- Area under velocity-time graph for distance.
- Echo path length first, then time.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q7, Q8, Q12, Q14, Q15, Q18.
- Physics: Q21, Q24, Q25, Q26, Q27, Q28, Q32, Q35, Q36.

---

### NSAA 2017

Maths pattern:
- More algebraic manipulation than 2016.
- Strong representation of proportionality, sequences, polygon geometry, bearings, clock angles, probability and 2D/3D geometry.

Best Maths techniques:
- Use exterior angle = 360/n for regular polygons.
- For clocks, use relative angular speed.
- For similar solids, volume scale = length scale cubed.
- For probability without replacement, write product explicitly.
- For coordinate parallelograms, use vector relationships before formulae.

Physics pattern:
- Excellent for graph interpretation, electricity, efficiency, nuclear balancing, density scaling, wave graphs, momentum and half-life.

Best Physics techniques:
- For velocity-time graphs, isolate decelerating sections only.
- For EM spectrum questions, use v = fλ.
- For nuclear equations, balance A and Z before reading options.
- For atom/nucleus density ratio, density scales as 1/radius³ if mass is fixed.
- For circuit power, use known resistor power to infer branch current/voltage.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q5, Q10, Q12, Q13, Q14, Q15, Q17, Q18.
- Physics: Q19, Q21, Q25, Q28, Q29, Q31, Q33, Q34, Q36.

---

### NSAA 2018

Maths pattern:
- Tables, direct proportionality, similar volumes, combined means, coordinate geometry, percentage multipliers, Venn-style counting, circles and estimation.

Best Maths techniques:
- For two groups and totals, table first.
- For similar cups/pillars, cube the scale factor.
- For combined means, use total sum, not average of averages.
- For geometry estimates, bracket the answer rather than exact solve if options are intervals.

Physics pattern:
- Strong circuit and mechanics year: series circuit removal, speed-time average, efficiency, conduction, KE/power, momentum, half-life background, terminal velocity, wave refraction, fission and V-I graph.

Best Physics techniques:
- For series removal, infer supply voltage from original current and resistance.
- For speed-time graphs, average speed = total area / total time.
- For half-life graphs, identify background before halving.
- For terminal speed, set weight = drag if acceleration is zero.
- For V-I graph plus series resistor, use supply voltage as the constraint line.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q4, Q6, Q7, Q8, Q14, Q15, Q16, Q18.
- Physics: Q20, Q22, Q24, Q27, Q28, Q31, Q32, Q34, Q35, Q36.

---

### NSAA 2019

Maths pattern:
- Algebra/surd manipulation, scaling, exponent laws, diagonals, transformations, conditional probability, bearings and pyramid geometry.

Best Maths techniques:
- Use difference of squares for surds.
- For sphere volume scaling, use (new radius / old radius)^3.
- For exponent equations, convert everything to one base.
- For transformations, move one vertex at a time.
- For statistics “must be true”, look for counterexamples.

Physics pattern:
- High ESAT relevance: waves, work/power, diode graphs, EM ratios, heating, Hooke’s law, gas pressure, transformers, latent heat, conduction, motor effect, nuclear sequences, KE/force, resistor networks, collisions and half-life.

Best Physics techniques:
- For wave amplitude questions, one oscillation distance = 4A.
- For diode plus resistor, read diode voltage from graph at given current.
- For gas compression at constant temperature, use pressure-volume relation.
- For motor effect balance readings, compare before/after difference.
- For collisions, compute initial momentum and energy separately.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q7, Q9, Q10, Q14, Q15, Q16, Q17, Q18.
- Physics: Q19, Q21, Q25, Q27, Q29, Q30, Q31, Q32, Q33, Q36.

### NSAA 2020

Maths pattern:
- New 20-question format. More emphasis on mixed short problems: sequences, simultaneous equations, probability, perpendicular lines, decay, circle geometry, inverse cube proportionality, statistics, dice, trig, transformations.

Best Maths techniques:
- Use complement for dice probability.
- For inverse cube, set up t = k/x³ immediately.
- For combined mean/range, mean is determined but range may only be bounded.
- For transformations, use midpoint tracking if asked for a translated segment.
- For tan x = 100x, graph shape and asymptotes beat algebra.

Physics pattern:
- Very strong ESAT-style Physics: thermal losses, ions/isotopes, gas compression, wave refraction, nuclear decay, insulation, transformers, transmission efficiency, thermistors, motor effect, EM spectrum, collisions, distance-time graphs, density packing, half-life plus background, terminal speed, drag work, springs and rocket acceleration.

Best Physics techniques:
- For thermal loss, input power × time minus mcΔT.
- For ideal gas at constant temperature, pressure and density scale together.
- For thermistors, infer fixed resistor from the first condition.
- For distance-time graph, use final constant gradient to infer speed.
- For rocket acceleration, constant force with decreasing mass means increasing acceleration.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q3, Q4, Q7, Q11, Q12, Q15, Q17, Q18, Q20.
- Physics: Q21, Q23, Q24, Q27, Q28, Q29, Q30, Q32, Q36, Q37, Q40.

### NSAA 2021

Maths pattern:
- Algebra and powers, tangent to circle, surface area comparison, patterns, ratios after equal spend, curve-line intersections, exact trig, circle theorem, probability, similar cuboids, relative speeds and unit conversion.

Best Maths techniques:
- Use tangent perpendicular to radius.
- For equal spending ratio questions, set initial amounts then subtract s.
- For q - p from roots, use discriminant/root separation rather than solving both roots.
- For relative-speed race questions, compare distances completed in same time.
- For fuel-cost unit conversion, write units through the expression.

Physics pattern:
- Stronger modern Physics style: power from charge/time, ideal gas density, transformer efficiency, towing, redshift, isotopes, springs, series-lamp power scaling, momentum-time graph, wave encounter, magnetic force, EM statements, multi-stage train kinematics, specific heat from graph gradient, drag and half-life ratios.

Best Physics techniques:
- Power = VQ/t is often faster than finding current first.
- For series lamps at fixed supply, total power ∝ 1/R_total.
- Momentum-time graph gradient = resultant force.
- For wave encounter, use relative speed.
- For graph gradient in heating, P = mc × gradient.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q4, Q8, Q10, Q11, Q12, Q14, Q15, Q17, Q20.
- Physics: Q21, Q23, Q24, Q28, Q29, Q30, Q31, Q34, Q35, Q36, Q40.

### NSAA 2022

Maths pattern:
- Algebraic simplification, right-triangle geometry, percentages, sequences, rational algebra, mean/median, inverse square proportionality, average speed, surface area, roots, similar triangles, polygon angles, translations and quadratic minima.

Best Maths techniques:
- For median and mean with repeated values, write the ordered list explicitly.
- For average speed uphill/downhill, use total distance and total time.
- For similar triangles, write proportional sides before substituting.
- For polygon angle chains, convert interior angle to 180 - 360/n.
- For quadratic minimum above x-axis, complete the square.

Physics pattern:
- Magnetic force, Doppler/echo, conduction, circuits, impulse, resistive work, ultrasound, efficiency, parallel/series with graphs, collision contact force, wave phase/displacement, isotopes, half-life from product-growth graph, calorimetry, induction and spring-energy scaling.

Best Physics techniques:
- For magnetic force, find current from charge/time first.
- For impulse, average force = change in momentum / time.
- For ultrasound, difference in return times gives distance difference after halving.
- For V-I graph circuits, read values at the actual current/voltage.
- For spring energy, E ∝ x² and height ∝ E/m.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q8, Q10, Q12, Q14, Q16, Q18, Q20.
- Physics: Q21, Q23, Q24, Q25, Q26, Q27, Q30, Q31, Q34, Q35, Q37.

### NSAA 2023

Maths pattern:
- Surface-area scaling, rearrangement, mean/median/mode, coordinate area, rational expressions, exponent equations, quadratic roots, percentage salary comparison, average speed, similar volumes, proportionality chains, scientific notation, vectors, arithmetic sequences, trig optimisation and probability transfer.

Best Maths techniques:
- For area in coordinate diagrams, use shoelace or split into simple triangles.
- For salary/percentage comparison, use multipliers from a common base.
- For similar solids, if area factor is 2 then length factor is √2 and volume factor is 2√2.
- For trig optimisation, set u = sin x and optimise the resulting expression.
- For transfer probability, condition on the first draw.

Physics pattern:
- Energy and speed from W/m, stopping distance, circuits, springs, nuclear beta, density/pressure, optics reflections, half-lives, falling-stone graph shape, conduction through layers, ice-water equilibrium, transformers, phase delay, particle motion in waves, magnetic forces on ring, floating blocks and gas compression.

Best Physics techniques:
- For stopping distance, use work-energy: force × distance = kinetic energy change.
- For layered conduction, temperature drop is steeper in the poorer conductor.
- For ice-water, check whether available heat is enough to melt all ice before calculating final temperature.
- For phase delay in glass, compare number of wavelengths fitting through same thickness.
- For floating block, use additional upthrust from extra displaced volume.

Priority questions:
- Maths: Q5, Q8, Q11, Q13, Q14, Q16, Q19, Q20.
- Physics: Q21, Q22, Q24, Q26, Q30, Q31, Q32, Q33, Q35, Q36, Q39, Q40.

## 7. Best cross-year quick techniques

### Top Maths techniques

1. Substitute answer options when rearrangement is ugly.
2. Use simple values to eliminate symbolic answers.
3. Convert percentages into multipliers.
4. Use convenient totals for ratio and probability.
5. Use complement for “at least one”.
6. Use area, volume and length scaling rules.
7. Redraw geometry cleanly.
8. Track transformations point by point.
9. Complete the square for quadratic minimum questions.
10. Sketch graphs when counting solutions.

### Top Physics techniques

1. Check units first.
2. Use proportionality before calculation.
3. Use graph meaning: gradient or area.
4. Reduce circuits before using formulae.
5. Use energy audits for efficiency and thermal questions.
6. Use A/Z ledgers for nuclear questions.
7. Use impulse and momentum signs for collisions.
8. Use v = fλ and remember frequency usually stays fixed across a boundary.
9. Use 4A per oscillation for particle distance in waves.
10. Use limiting cases for thermal, gas and circuit questions.

1. NSAA 2016 Part A and Part B: baseline technique drill.
2. NSAA 2017 Part A and Part B: algebra, graph and nuclear discipline.
3. NSAA 2018 Part A and Part B: circuits, graphs, half-life and mechanics.
4. NSAA 2019 Part A and Part B: strongest mixed ESAT-style set.
5. NSAA 2020 Part A and Part B: stricter 20-question timing.
6. NSAA 2021 Part A and Part B: modern graph, circuit and energy questions.
